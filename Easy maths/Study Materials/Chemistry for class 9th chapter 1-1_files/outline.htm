States of Matter
  • There are five states of matter:
  • 1. Solids
  • 2. Liquids
  • 3. Gases
  • 4. Plasmas and
  • 5. Bose-Einstein condensate(BEC)
12
1. Solids
  • The matter which have definite shape, distinct boundaries and fixed volumes, that is, have negligible compressibility.
  • For examples:
  • a pen, a book, a needle, a taable and a piece of wooden  stick.
13
1. Solids
  • The particles of solids are very closed to each other.

2. Liquids
  • The matter which have no definite (or a fixed) shape, but which have definite(or a fixed) volume.
  • They take up the shape of the container in which they are kept.
  • For examples:


  • water, cooking oil, milk, juice, and  a cold drink.

2. Liquids

3. Gases
  • The matter which have no difinite (or a fixed) shape and no definite (or a fixed) volume.

  • Air, Hellium, liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) and
  • Compressed natural gas (CNG), etc.

Changing States of Matter
or phase changes

Note: The five states of matter is also called Phase.
29
Types of changing of Matter.
  • There are two types of changing the phase of the matter
  • Physical changing: Physical changes are usually about physical states of matter.
  • For example, changing ice into water. And again water to ice.


  • Chemical changing: Chemical changes happen when atomic bonds are broken or created during chemical reactions.
  • For example, The formation of curd from milk.
30
1. Effect of change of temperature


  • On increasing the temperature of solids, the kinetic energy of the particles increases. Due to the increase in kinetic energy, the particles start vibrating with greater speed.
31
Melting Points
  • The minimum temperature at which solid melts to become a liquid at the atmospheric pressure is called its melting point.


  • Note:- The melting point of a solid is an indication of the strength of the force of attraction between its particles.
  • The melting point of ice is 273.15 K
32
What is fusion?


  • The process of melting, that is, change of solid state into liquid state is also known as fusion.
33
What is Latent heat?
  • The amount of heat energy that is required to change 1 kg of a solid into liquid at atmospheric pressure at its melting point is known as the latent heat of fusion.
34
What is the boiling point?
  • The temperature at which a liquid starts boiling at the atmospheric pressure is known as its boiling point.
  • The boiling point of water is 100-degree Celsius.
  • For water this temperature is 373 K (100 0C = 273 + 100 = 373 K).
  • Note: Kelvin is the SI unit of temperature, 00 C =273.15 K.
  • we take 00 C = 273 K after rounding off the decimal.
35
Sublimation and deposition
  • A change of state directly from solid to gas without changing into a liquid state is called sublimation and the direct change of a gas to solid without changing into liquid is called deposition.
36
2. Effect of change of pressure

  • carbon dioxide (CO2) is stored under high pressure. Solid CO2 gets converted directly to gaseous state on the decrease of pressure to 1 atmosphere* without coming into a liquid state. This is the reason that solid carbon dioxide is also known as dry ice.

Evaporation

  • In the case of liquids, a small fraction of particles at the surface, having higher kinetic energy, is able to break away from the forces of attraction of other particles and gets converted into vapour. This phenomenon of change of a liquid into vapours at any temperature below its boiling point is called evaporation.
43
Factors affecting evaporation
  • an increase of surface area


  • an increase of temperature


  •  • a decrease in humidity


  • an increase in wind speed

Q). Why should we wear cotton clothes in summer?

  • Answer: In summer we wear cotton clothes because cotton is a good absorber of sweat from our body and exposing to the atmosphere for easy evaporation. By the help of evaporation, cotton absorbs sweat and keep our body cool.
